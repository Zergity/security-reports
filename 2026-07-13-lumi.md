# Lumi Finance (Sodium ERC-4337 Wallet) Validation-Time Approval Exploit — July 2026

**Report date:** 2026-07-15
**Incident date:** 2026-07-13
**Target:** Lumi Finance / Lumiterra — the **Sodium** ERC-4337 smart-account wallet (`@Lumi_Finance`)
**Loss:** ~$264K reported by SlowMist; ~$270K in Blockaid-flagged press. On-chain, the attacker pulled **74,480.72 LUAUSD + 24,656.74 USDT + 1,149.37 USDC.e + 47.63 USDC (≈ $100.3K stablecoin-equivalent) + 65,964.43 LUA** out of **3,383 distinct victim smart accounts**; the gap to the ~$264–270K headline is the LUA leg valued at its pre-hack price (LUA has since collapsed to ~$0.16 with ~$50 of liquidity).
**Category:** ERC-4337 account-abstraction flaw — the Sodium account grants an ERC-20 **approval as a side effect during `validateUserOp` (the validation phase)**, to an attacker-controlled "paymaster," for an attacker-chosen token and an attacker-dictated amount, with no allowlist of trusted paymasters. Reaching that side-effect requires passing the account's signature check, which the attacker did through Sodium's `executeWithSodiumAuthSession` path — it treats a caller-supplied session key as authoritative when the account has no "safe session" configured (the default).
**Chain:** Arbitrum One (EntryPoint v0.6 `0x5ff1…2789`)
**Status:** Blockaid and SlowMist flagged the campaign on 2026-07-13; users urged to revoke approvals to the malicious contracts (Revoke.cash). No Lumi recovery/compensation plan published at the time of writing. Attacker swapped stablecoins to ETH and laundered 12 ETH through Tornado Cash.

> Scope note: This is an internal reference report. The two-phase harvest→drain structure (§3.3–§3.4), the proof that all approvals fired **during** the validation phase (§3.3), the campaign totals and 3,383-victim count (§4), the five attacker-deployed sweeper/helper contracts (§4, §7), and the laundering path including the 12-ETH Tornado Cash deposits (§5) were reconstructed first-hand from Arbitrum chain data on 2026-07-15 — transaction receipts and their `Approval`/`Transfer`/`UserOperationEvent`/`BeforeExecution` logs, ERC-20 token-transfer aggregation across the sink contracts, calldata decoding, and historical `eth_call`/storage reads (Etherscan V2 API, chainid 42161). The root-cause reading (§3.2, §3.5) is grounded in Lumi's own **verified** `Sodium` account source (`0xb5bc…6b8b`). Two links are deduced rather than directly traced and are flagged as such: the exact `executeWithSodiumAuthSession` selector inside each UserOp (no public Arbitrum node exposed a transaction trace for a bundle this large), and the USD valuation of the LUA/LUAUSD legs (no timestamped price source for 2026-07-13). Off-chain context — the ~$264K/~$270K headline, Blockaid detection, and the "no recovery plan" status — is as reported by SlowMist and press and is flagged where it goes beyond what the chain shows.

## 1. Executive summary

On 2026-07-13 an attacker drained roughly a quarter-million dollars from **3,383 Lumi "Sodium" smart-account wallets** on Arbitrum. Sodium is an ERC-4337 account: users don't send transactions directly, they sign *UserOperations* that a bundler feeds to the canonical `EntryPoint`, which validates and then executes each one.

The flaw is in what Sodium's account does *during validation*. Sodium supports paying gas in ERC-20 tokens via a paymaster, and to make that work its `validateUserOp` calls an internal helper, `_approvePaymasterToken`, that reads a paymaster address, a token address, and an approval amount out of the UserOperation's `paymasterAndData` field and then makes the account call `token.approve(paymaster, amount)`. Every one of those three inputs is attacker-controlled, and there is **no check that the paymaster is a legitimate Lumi paymaster**. So an attacker who submits a UserOperation naming *their own contract* as the paymaster, *any token the victim holds* as the token, and *max-uint256* as the amount, walks away with an unlimited allowance on that token — granted by the victim's own account, during validation, without the victim ever signing an approval.

To reach that helper the attacker still had to get past the account's signature check. Sodium exposes an execution path, `executeWithSodiumAuthSession`, whose validation logic accepts a session key **supplied by the caller in the UserOperation's own calldata** as long as the signature matches that same key and the account has no "safe session" configured — the default state for an ordinary user. There is no check that the key is authorized on the account. The attacker set the session key to an address they controlled, signed with the matching key, and validation returned "valid." Because the attacker ran their own bundler (they called the `EntryPoint` directly through their contract), the standard ERC-4337 simulation rules that forbid an account from touching external contracts during validation — the rules designed to catch exactly this — never applied.

The attack ran in two passes. **Pass one (harvest):** a batch of UserOperations that pass validation, granting the attacker's contract a max allowance on each victim's tokens as a validation side-effect. The operations' *execution* then reverts — which does not matter, because the approval was already banked during validation. **Pass two (drain):** the attacker's contract, now an approved spender, calls `transferFrom` on every victim for every token and sweeps the balances. Stolen stablecoins were swapped to ETH; LUA and LUAUSD were sent to the attacker's wallet; 12 ETH was pushed through Tornado Cash.

The load-bearing on-chain fact is the log ordering (§3.3): in the harvest transaction, all 117 `Approval` events land **before** the `EntryPoint`'s single `BeforeExecution()` marker, i.e. entirely within the validation phase, and each corresponding `UserOperationEvent` carries `success = false`. Value was extracted in validation; execution was a discarded no-op.

## 3. Attack vector and root cause

### 3.1 The system

- **Sodium account** — Lumi/Lumiterra's ERC-4337 smart-account wallet. Each user account is a thin proxy (384-byte runtime) that stores its implementation at the storage slot keyed by *its own address* and `delegatecall`s into the shared **`Sodium`** singleton implementation `0xb5bc46df04dee31d219e7664122e29eed9506b8b` (verified source, Solidity 0.8.17). The account stores `salt = keccak256(owner)` rather than the owner address; there is no public `owner()` getter.
- **EntryPoint v0.6** `0x5ff137d4b0fdcd49dca30c7cf57e578a026d2789` — the canonical ERC-4337 singleton that runs `handleOps`.
- **Target tokens** (the five the attacker harvested): USDT `0xfd08…fcbb9`, USDC (native) `0xaf88…5831`, USDC.e (bridged) `0xff97…5cc8`, and Lumi's own **LUA** `0xc3ab…1c38` and **LUAUSD** `0x1dd6…636c`.

### 3.2 The two flaws that combine

**Flaw A — approval as a validation side-effect (the value extraction).** In `Sodium.sol`, `validateUserOp` ends by calling `_approvePaymasterToken(userOp)`:

```solidity
function _approvePaymasterToken(UserOperation calldata userOp) private {
    bytes calldata paymasterAndData = userOp.paymasterAndData;
    if (paymasterAndData.length != 20 + 4 + 20) {
        return;
    } else if (bytes4(paymasterAndData[20:24]) == bytes4(0x095ea7b3)) {          // "approve" marker
        IERC20Paymaster paymaster = IERC20Paymaster(address(bytes20(paymasterAndData[:20])));   // attacker-controlled
        IERC20Metadata  payToken  = IERC20Metadata(address(bytes20(paymasterAndData[24:])));    // attacker-controlled
        (uint256 miniAllowance, uint256 suggestApproveValue) = paymaster.getTokenAllowanceCast(payToken);  // calls attacker contract
        if (payToken.allowance(address(this), address(paymaster)) < miniAllowance) {
            payToken.approve(address(paymaster), suggestApproveValue);            // account approves attacker
        }
    }
}

function validateUserOp(UserOperation calldata userOp, bytes32 userOpHash, uint256 missingAccountFunds)
    external override returns (uint256 validationData)
{
    _requireFromEntryPoint();
    validationData = _validateSignature(userOp, userOpHash);
    _validateNonce(userOp.nonce);
    _payPrefund(missingAccountFunds);
    _approvePaymasterToken(userOp);            // <-- side-effect, runs during the validation phase
}
```

The intended feature is a pre-approval so a legitimate ERC-20 paymaster can pull its gas fee. But the paymaster address, the token, and even the amount are all taken from attacker-supplied fields — `paymasterAndData[:20]` (paymaster), `paymasterAndData[24:44]` (token), and `suggestApproveValue`, which the account fetches by *calling the paymaster itself* (`getTokenAllowanceCast`). There is no allowlist of trusted paymasters, no cap on the amount, and no restriction on which token can be approved. The attacker's contract returns a huge `miniAllowance` (forcing the `<` branch) and `suggestApproveValue = 2²⁵⁶−1`, and the victim account approves the attacker's contract for an unlimited allowance on whichever token the attacker named.

**Flaw B — caller-supplied session key accepted as authoritative (the signature bypass).** `_approvePaymasterToken` is only reached if `validateUserOp` returns `0` (valid); otherwise the `EntryPoint` reverts the whole bundle with `AA24 signature error`. Sodium's `_validateSignature` dispatches on the UserOperation's call selector. Most paths require the recovered signer to already be an authorized owner/session (`isSessionOwner(signer)`), which the attacker cannot satisfy for strangers' accounts. But the `executeWithSodiumAuthSession` path does not:

```solidity
if (methodId == this.executeWithSodiumAuthSession.selector) {
    address sessionKey;                          // read from userOp.callData[16:36] — attacker-controlled
    bytes memory addressBytes = userOp.callData[16:36];
    assembly { sessionKey := mload(add(addressBytes, 20)) }

    bool isSafe = _safeSession.owner == address(0);                 // TRUE for accounts with no safe session (default)
    IUserOperationValidator validator = internalReadUserOperationValidator();
    if (!isSafe) { isSafe = validator.validateUserOp(userOp) < 2; }
    return sessionKey == signer && isSafe ? 0 : 1;                  // no check that sessionKey is authorized on this account
}
```

The session key is read from the attacker's own calldata; `signer` is recovered from the attacker's own signature. Set both to an address the attacker controls and `sessionKey == signer` holds trivially. For any account that never configured a "safe session" — the default — `isSafe` is `true` and the on-chain validator is never even consulted. Validation returns `0`. The check never verifies that this key has any relationship to the account.

**Why self-bundling matters.** ERC-4337 forbids an account's validation from calling external contracts or reading external storage precisely so that a validation side-effect like Flaw A cannot happen; those rules are enforced by *bundlers* during off-chain simulation, not by the `EntryPoint` on-chain. The attacker was their own bundler — every `handleOps` call came from an attacker contract — so no simulation ever screened these operations.

### 3.3 The harvest transaction — approvals happen in the validation phase

Example: `0x630654fb…6604` (block 483,389,834), a single `handleOps` bundle. Its 235 logs decode to exactly three things, in this order:

| Log range | Event | Count |
|---|---|---|
| #0 – #116 | `Approval(victim → sweeper 0x5636…e8a1, value = 2²⁵⁶−1)` | 117 |
| #117 | `EntryPoint.BeforeExecution()` | 1 |
| #118 – #234 | `EntryPoint.UserOperationEvent(...)`, each `paymaster = 0x5636…e8a1`, `success = false` | 117 |

In EntryPoint v0.6 `handleOps`, the entire validation loop runs first, then a single `BeforeExecution()` is emitted, then the execution loop runs. **Every `Approval` is at a log index below `BeforeExecution()`** — proof that all 117 approvals were granted during validation, not execution. The paired `UserOperationEvent`s all report `success = false`: the operations' *execution* reverted, yet the allowances persisted, because ERC-4337 commits validation-phase state changes even when the subsequent execution fails. There are **zero `Transfer` events** in this transaction: the harvest pass moves no tokens, it only collects approvals.

The calldata to the sweeper (selector `0x363e464b`) is just a list of **50 victim addresses** plus a list of **5 token addresses** (USDT, LUA, LUAUSD, USDC, USDC.e). The sweeper builds one approval-harvesting UserOperation per (victim, token-held) pair internally — 50 accounts producing 117 operations because it skips tokens a given account doesn't hold.

### 3.4 The drain transaction

Example: `0x020995ec…ea90` (block 483,390,715), three minutes later (selector `0x96e676e5`). This one carries **117 `Transfer` events** — `transferFrom(victim, sweeper, balance)` for each token of a *different* batch of 53 accounts — plus in-transaction swaps: the sweeper approves a router (`0x68b3…fc45`) and sells the harvested stablecoins into Uniswap V3 pools for WETH, while LUA and LUAUSD are forwarded to the attacker EOA. The harvest and drain batches are disjoint (the two example transactions share zero victims), which is why the campaign needed many transactions of each type.

### 3.5 Root cause — one line each

- **Extraction:** an ERC-4337 account that performs `token.approve(paymaster, amount)` during `validateUserOp`, with the paymaster, token, and amount all taken from unauthenticated, attacker-controlled `paymasterAndData`/paymaster-callback inputs and no allowlist of trusted paymasters.
- **Reachability:** a validation path (`executeWithSodiumAuthSession`) that accepts a caller-supplied session key as authoritative — `sessionKey == signer` with no check that the key is bound to the account — whenever the account has no safe session configured.
- **Absence of the usual guardrail:** the attacker self-bundled, so ERC-4337's off-chain validation rules (which prohibit external calls during account validation) never screened the operations.

### 3.6 Ruled out (on-chain evidence)

- **Owner-key / private-key compromise:** ruled out. Victim accounts have a real owner (`salt() = keccak256(owner)` is set on the sampled account) and the attacker did not use it — validation passed through the caller-supplied-session-key path, not owner authentication.
- **A bug in the `EntryPoint` or in ERC-4337 itself:** none. The canonical v0.6 `EntryPoint` behaved exactly as specified — it committed validation-phase state and discarded reverted executions. The flaw is entirely in the Sodium account's `validateUserOp`.
- **Loss from a Lumi protocol/treasury contract:** no. Funds came out of individual user smart accounts via standard ERC-20 `approve`/`transferFrom`; no Lumi pool or vault was touched.
- **Execution-phase exploitation:** ruled out by the log ordering (§3.3) — the value-bearing side-effect is entirely in validation, and every operation's execution reverted.

## 5. Laundering

Within the drain transactions the harvested stablecoins were swapped to WETH through Uniswap V3 pools (`0x641c…c79d`, `0xc31e…a443`, `0xc696…e8d0`) and unwrapped to ETH; LUA and LUAUSD were forwarded to the attacker EOA. On 2026-07-13 between 11:48:14 and 11:55:05 UTC the EOA deposited **12 ETH in twelve 1-ETH tranches into Tornado Cash** through the `TornadoProxyLight` router `0x0d5550d52428e7e3175bfc9550207e4ad3859b17` (deposit selector `0x13d98d13`). The Tornado Cash leg is a first-hand on-chain finding not present in the public reporting, which described only "swapped into ETH and transferred to the attacker's wallet." Onward movement past Tornado is out of scope.

## 6. Root-cause summary and remediation

**Root cause.** An ERC-4337 smart account (`Sodium`) that (1) performs an ERC-20 `approve` to an attacker-controlled paymaster, for an attacker-chosen token, at an attacker-dictated amount, **as a side effect of `validateUserOp`**, with no allowlist of trusted paymasters and no amount cap; and (2) lets an unauthorized caller pass validation via `executeWithSodiumAuthSession` by supplying their own session key when the account has no safe session set. Self-bundling removed the ERC-4337 simulation rules that would otherwise have rejected the operations.

**What would fix it:**

- **Do not grant approvals during validation, and never to an unvetted paymaster.** If ERC-20 gas payment is required, restrict the paymaster to a hard-coded allowlist of Lumi paymaster addresses, approve only the specific gas token, and bound the amount to a small, computed fee — never `2²⁵⁶−1`, and never an amount the paymaster itself dictates.
- **Remove attacker-controlled inputs from the approval decision.** The token and amount must come from account policy, not from `paymasterAndData` and not from a value the paymaster returns via `getTokenAllowanceCast`.
- **Fix the session-key check.** `executeWithSodiumAuthSession` must verify the session key is actually registered on the account (and unexpired), regardless of whether a "safe session" exists. A caller-supplied key matching a caller-supplied signature proves nothing about authorization.
- **Keep validation side-effect-free (ERC-4337 spirit).** Validation should only verify; any state change belongs in execution, where a reverting operation rolls it back. A side-effect that survives a failed execution is a red flag by construction.
- **User mitigation, now:** revoke all allowances to `0x5636…e8a1`, `0x8c6f…e2d9`, `0xf209…b110`, `0x9698…69ce` (and screen for others sharing the 13,420-byte bytecode) via Revoke.cash or an equivalent.

**Broader lesson.** In account abstraction, "validation" and "execution" are different trust domains: the `EntryPoint` commits validation-phase state even when execution reverts, and off-chain bundler rules — not the chain — are what normally stop an account from doing anything consequential during validation. An account that mutates token allowances inside `validateUserOp` has effectively moved a privileged action outside every guardrail that protects it, and a self-bundling attacker pays nothing to exploit that.
