# BonkDAO Governance-Capture Treasury Drain — July 2026

**Report date:** 2026-07-07
**Incident date:** 2026-07-06 (malicious proposal BIP #76 created 2026-06-30, executed 2026-07-06)
**Target:** BonkDAO — the BONK community treasury, governed on Solana via Realms / SPL Governance
**Loss:** 4,426,104,450,305.97 BONK (~$19.3M at the moment of transfer; press figures ~$20M)
**Category:** Governance attack — token-weighted vote capture. An attacker bought enough BONK on the open market to clear the DAO's 1%-of-supply quorum, then passed a proposal whose executable instruction transferred the treasury's entire BONK balance to a wallet it controlled. No smart-contract flaw was exploited; the SPL Governance program executed exactly as designed.
**Chain:** Solana mainnet
**Status:** Funds largely static as of the report date. ~4.386 trillion BONK (~$19M) sits unmoved at a second-hop wallet (`EXaJ…eh42`); ~40 billion BONK (~$180K) was routed onward through an exchange-bound address. Treasury BONK account emptied to dust. Recovery coordination with exchanges and the Solana Foundation is ongoing.

> Scope note: The governance topology (§3), the vote tally and quorum arithmetic (§3.3), the proposal's on-chain title/description and its embedded instruction list (§3.2), the execution transactions and the full BONK flow (§4), and the current balances (§4.3) were verified first-hand against Solana mainnet on 2026-07-07. Those findings are first-hand. Off-chain context — the attacker's exchange purchases and DeFi borrowing (attributed to Lookonchain), the exchange trading pauses (Upbit, Kraken), the price move, and BonkDAO's own statements — is as-reported and flagged where unconfirmed.

## 1. Executive summary

On 2026-07-06 the BonkDAO treasury lost its entire BONK holding — 4,426,104,450,305.97 BONK, worth roughly $19–20 million — not to a code exploit but to its own voting rules. BonkDAO governs the BONK community treasury through Solana's SPL Governance program (the Realms stack). Voting power is proportional to BONK held, and a proposal passes once "yes" weight exceeds a quorum set at **1% of the token's supply**. With BONK's on-chain supply at 87,994,713,371,682.94, that quorum is 879,947,133,716.83 BONK — about $4 million at the time.

The attacker spent roughly $4–4.4 million over several days (reportedly buying on Bybit and Binance and borrowing more through DeFi lending, per Lookonchain) to assemble a BONK position just large enough to clear that bar. On 2026-06-30 they filed **"BIP # 76 - Sowellian BonkDAO,"** a proposal whose on-chain description states its purpose plainly — *"Implement Sowellian governance… monetize holdings, stop the bleeding, all YES voters are eligible to receive tokens"* — and whose executable body is two instructions: add token metadata, then *"Send 4.426.104.450.305 Bonk to 9bxWkNf3BtJ6iehq9KbX9uCWMjem4TFiPZ19T2sYJHvQ."* The "all YES voters eligible to receive tokens" line was bait to draw additional favourable votes.

The proposal ran the DAO's standard six-day voting window and closed on 2026-07-06 08:25:39 UTC with an "Approve" weight of **882,383,387,283.79 BONK** — clearing the 879.95-billion quorum by about 2.44 billion BONK, a margin of roughly 0.28%, almost exactly the stake the attacker had spent the week accumulating. Reporting puts turnout at seven wallets voting yes against more than 18,000 members who did not (about 2.9%); the yes weight is essentially the attacker's own assembled position, so the vote was, in effect, one party agreeing with itself.

Forty-nine seconds after voting closed, at 08:26:28 UTC, the proposal executed. There is no hold-up period between a passing vote and execution, so no window existed for the community or a guardian to intervene. Execution ran through the SPL Governance program's `ExecuteTransaction` path: it created the recipient's BONK token account, then invoked an SPL-Token transfer moving 4,426,104,450,305.97 BONK from the treasury's token account (`F8FqZu…`, authority the governance treasury PDA `AGkGWK1R669…`) to the attacker's wallet `9bxWkNf3…JHvQ`. The treasury BONK account now holds dust.

Later the same day the attacker moved 40 billion BONK (~$180K) through `Dd4vDG5…` — consistent with the ~$188K that reporting says reached an exchange about nine hours after the drain — and swept the remaining ~4.386 trillion BONK (~$19M) to a second wallet, `EXaJ…eh42`, where it still sits. Because the bulk of the loss is parked on-chain and has not been laundered, a recovery window remains open, which is why BonkDAO's response has centred on exchange and Solana Foundation coordination rather than post-mortem forensics.

The root cause is economic, not technical: BonkDAO let voting power be bought on the open market while pricing a treasury-emptying proposal at a quorum (~$4M) far below the treasury it could authorize spending from (~$20M), and shipped no timelock or veto to catch the outcome. The six-day voting period defends against flash-loan governance attacks (the Beanstalk pattern) but does nothing against an attacker willing to hold real tokens for a week.

## 3. Attack vector and root cause

### 3.1 What BonkDAO is and how it decides

BonkDAO is a Realms DAO built on Solana's SPL Governance program (`GovER5Lthms3bLBqWub97yVrMmEogzX7xNjdXpPPCVZw`). The relevant on-chain accounts, all verified first-hand:

- **Realm** `84pGFuy1Y27ApK67ApethaPvexeDWA66zNV8gm38TVeQ` — `RealmV2`, name **"Bonk DAO"**, community mint = BONK (`DezXAZ8z7PnrnRJjz3wXBoRgixCa6xjnB7YaB1pPB263`).
- **Governance** `Uq5BRkVfdBpMknZJHw6huS3dunEgJpUDv3M2DG3BfQg` — `GovernanceV2`, governed account = the BONK mint. This is the governance that owns the treasury and whose proposals can move it.
- **Treasury authority** `AGkGWK1R669KDT4FCqgDgK7PgahGJPjD4J9xmVjuL9kn` — the governance's native-treasury PDA, i.e. the on-chain signer that holds and moves treasury assets when a proposal executes.
- **Treasury BONK account** `F8FqZuUKfoy58aHLW6bfeEhfW9sTtJyqFTqnxVmGZ6dU` — the associated token account (owned by the PDA above) that held the treasury's BONK. It is the source of the drained funds.

Voting is token-weighted: one BONK, one vote. A community proposal succeeds when "Approve" weight crosses the quorum, which BonkDAO set at **1% of BONK supply**. There is no evidence in the executed sequence of any post-vote hold-up (the transfer executed 49 seconds after the vote closed), and no council veto blocked it.

### 3.2 The malicious proposal (as recorded on-chain)

The `ProposalV2` account `6wR1jdhhJ31bbdRNXva8MxqsgsNLKTxargcdAyZ7FcRj` carries the proposal's own text:

```
name        : "BIP # 76 - Sowellian BonkDAO"
description : "<p>Implement Sowellian governance, install new members and council,
               rebuild from the ashes, monetize holdings, stop the bleeding,
               all YES voters are eligible to receive tokens</p>"
instructions: "<p>1. Add Metadata</p>
               <p>2. Send 4.426.104.450.305 Bonk to
                   9bxWkNf3BtJ6iehq9KbX9uCWMjem4TFiPZ19T2sYJHvQ</p>"
state byte  : 5  (Completed)
```

The proposal is self-incriminating: its description names the exact payout — 4,426,104,450,305 BONK — and the exact destination wallet, and dangles a reward to anyone voting yes. The "install new members and council… rebuild from the ashes" framing is social cover; the mechanical effect is a single treasury transfer to an attacker-controlled address.

Two `ProposalTransaction` accounts hold the executable instructions: `FMe1f7weHQ83Mvj9TEjTZGPdesm1m2f1uUprwXGUgRyM` (create the recipient token account / metadata) and `5y9dZT4nELdqrpQY4Zfm3ZUXgeANr5tABB8vphkKv2u7` (the BONK transfer).

### 3.3 The vote — bought quorum, razor-thin margin

The whole attack turns on one arithmetic relationship, and every term in it is verifiable on-chain:

| Quantity | Value (BONK) | Source |
|---|---|---|
| BONK total supply | 87,994,713,371,682.94 | on-chain mint supply |
| Quorum (1% of supply) | 879,947,133,716.83 | supply ÷ 100 |
| "Approve" weight on BIP #76 | 882,383,387,283.79 | recorded on the `ProposalV2` account |
| Margin over quorum | 2,436,253,566.96 (~0.28%) | difference |

The reported round figures — "882.38 billion in favor against an 879.95 billion threshold" — match the on-chain values to the decimal. The attacker sized the buy to just clear 1% of supply and no more: at BONK's price that quorum was about $4 million. Reporting puts participation at seven wallets voting yes versus 18,000+ members who did not (turnout ~2.9%); given that the yes weight equals the position the attacker spent the week assembling, the "99.9% yes" outcome is effectively one voter ratifying its own proposal, with a handful of small votes likely drawn by the "yes voters eligible to receive tokens" incentive.

Voting timestamps recorded on the proposal account: opened 2026-06-30 08:25:39 UTC, closed 2026-07-06 08:25:39 UTC — a six-day window — with execution at 08:26:28, forty-nine seconds after close.

### 3.4 Root cause — a governance economics failure, not a bug

Nothing on-chain malfunctioned. The SPL Governance program tallied a legitimate token-weighted vote, confirmed the quorum was met, and executed the proposal's instructions exactly as written. The "vulnerability" is in BonkDAO's governance parameters and the economic invariant they failed to hold:

1. **Voting power is purchasable.** BONK trades freely, so voting weight equals money. Any actor can buy governance influence on the open market — there is no stake-locking, reputation, or identity gate.
2. **Quorum was far cheaper than the treasury it controlled.** A proposal that could move the entire BONK treasury (~$20M) needed only ~1% of supply (~$4M) of "yes" weight to pass. The cost to seize the treasury was roughly a fifth of its value. A sound design requires the cost of passing a spending proposal to exceed the value that proposal can extract; here it was 5× cheaper to attack than the prize.
3. **No post-vote timelock.** Execution followed the vote by 49 seconds. A hold-up (cool-down) period between a passing vote and execution is the standard defence that gives honest participants and guardians time to react — to rally counter-votes, alert exchanges, or trigger an emergency freeze. BonkDAO had none in this path.
4. **No guardian veto or spending cap.** No council multisig could block or delay a treasury-draining proposal, and a single proposal was allowed to move 100% of the treasury's BONK in one instruction.
5. **Chronic low turnout.** With ~2.9% participation, the honest majority was absent, and a 1% quorum meant their absence was decisive. Low quorums are set to make governance *function* under apathy; the same setting makes it *capturable* under apathy.

The six-day voting period deserves a specific note. It is the classic defence against flash-loan governance attacks — the 2022 Beanstalk drain worked because a proposal could be proposed, voted, and executed inside one flash-loaned block, so borrowed voting power sufficed. BonkDAO's multi-day window defeats that: you cannot flash-borrow voting weight and hold it for a week. But it is no defence against **patient capital**. An attacker who is willing to actually buy and hold the tokens for the voting period pays only the market cost of the quorum stake, which here was cheap relative to the treasury and which they could partly recoup by selling the stake afterward. The design hardened against the previous era's attack and left the door open for the simpler one.

### 3.5 Why this is not a smart-contract exploit

It is worth stating plainly, because the distinction drives the remediation. In the shielded-pool and MEV incidents catalogued elsewhere in this repository, an attacker made the code do something its authors did not intend. Here the attacker made the DAO do exactly what its rules permit: a majority of token weight voted to send tokens somewhere, and the program obeyed. The failure is that "a majority of token weight" was for sale at a fifth of the price of the treasury, and that nothing stood between the vote and the payout. Auditing the Solidity/Rust would not have found this; the flaw lives in the governance configuration and the token's own market liquidity.

## 5. Impact

- **Direct loss:** 4,426,104,450,305.97 BONK, the treasury's entire BONK position — ~$19.3M at transfer, ~$20M in press figures.
- **Who bears it:** the BONK community treasury and, indirectly, BONK holders. BonkDAO reportedly held on the order of 15% of available BONK; losing a large treasury slug is both a direct hit and a confidence shock.
- **Market:** BONK fell roughly 7–10% on the news (market cap around $400M) *(as reported)*. Upbit and Kraken paused BONK deposits and withdrawals, Upbit citing user-protection measures around a security incident *(as reported)*.
- **Recoverability:** better than typical. ~$19M remains static on-chain at `EXaJ…eh42`; only ~$180K has left via an exchange path. Any recovery depends on exchange cooperation, on the attacker eventually needing an off-ramp, and on whether the BONK issuer/DAO pursues token-level measures — none guaranteed, but the funds are not yet gone.

## 7. Remediation and recommendations

The defect is in governance economics and configuration, so the fixes are governance-design changes, not a code patch. Ordered by impact.

### 7.1 Immediate
- **Contain the identified address.** Circulate `EXaJ…eh42` (and `9bxWkNf3…JHvQ`, `Dd4vDG5…`) to exchanges and analytics providers to block deposits and flag movement while the funds remain static.
- **Freeze or gate the treasury governance.** Realm authorities can adjust governance config or move remaining non-BONK treasury assets under a temporary multisig so a second proposal cannot repeat the drain on other assets.

### 7.2 The core fixes — make the treasury cost more to seize than it holds
- **Add a post-vote timelock (hold-up period).** This is the single most valuable change. A mandatory delay (days, scaled to proposal value) between a passing vote and execution gives the community and guardians time to counter-vote, alert exchanges, or trigger a freeze. Forty-nine seconds is not a governance process; it is a payout.
- **Raise quorum/approval thresholds above the value at risk, and scale them to what a proposal can spend.** A proposal that can move the whole treasury should require far more than 1% of supply. Tie the required "yes" weight (and/or a supermajority) to the size of the transfer, so seizing the treasury costs more than the treasury is worth. The invariant to restore: cost-to-pass ≥ value-at-risk.
- **Cap what a single proposal can move.** Per-proposal and per-epoch spending limits on treasury governance turn a total drain into a bounded, recoverable loss and force large disbursements through multiple, separately reviewable votes.

### 7.3 Guardian and review layer
- **Council / multisig veto over treasury-spending proposals.** A trusted council able to veto or delay disbursement proposals (Realms supports council + community token configurations) catches an obviously hostile instruction set before it executes. Pair it with an emergency freeze authority.
- **Whitelist instruction targets.** Restrict treasury-governance proposals to transferring to pre-approved addresses or program instructions, so "send the whole treasury to a fresh EOA" is not a valid executable proposal in the first place.
- **Signatory / proposal-review gating.** Require signatories or a review step for proposals whose executable body moves large balances, rather than letting any token holder queue an arbitrary transfer.

### 7.4 Monitoring
- Alert on any proposal whose executable instructions transfer a large fraction of the treasury to a non-whitelisted address — this proposal advertised its own payout and destination in plain text and still executed.
- Alert on rapid accumulation of the governance token by a wallet that then proposes or votes — the days-long buy-up here was visible on-chain before the vote closed.
- Watch the quorum-to-treasury ratio as a standing risk metric: whenever the market cost of quorum drops below the treasury value, the DAO is economically capturable.

## 8. Broader context

This is a governance attack, structurally distinct from the code exploits elsewhere in this repository. Its ancestor is the April 2022 Beanstalk drain, which used a flash loan to borrow voting power for a single block. The industry response — multi-day voting periods — closed the flash-loan door, and BonkDAO had that door closed. What it did not close was the plain-capital version: an attacker who simply buys and holds the governance token through the voting window, exploiting a quorum priced far below the treasury and the absence of any delay or veto after the vote passes.

Meme-coin DAOs are unusually exposed to this. They combine large treasuries, a freely and cheaply tradable governance token, low quorums tuned for apathetic participation, and thin turnout — every ingredient the attack needs. The uncomfortable lesson is that token-weighted governance without an economic floor is a market, and any treasury worth more than its quorum is for sale. The near-term saving grace here is only that the proceeds are still parked on-chain; the design that allowed the theft is unchanged until the parameters are.
