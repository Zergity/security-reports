## 1. Executive summary

On 2026-07-03, an attacker drained the Hinkal Protocol shielded pool on Ethereum. Hinkal is a privacy protocol that holds user deposits in a pool and lets users withdraw through zero-knowledge (Groth16) proofs, so on-chain observers cannot link a deposit to a withdrawal.

The attacker abused Hinkal's `prooflessDeposit` function. That function accepts a deposit and inserts a user-supplied commitment (a shielded note) into the pool without requiring a validity proof that binds the note's hidden value to the amount actually deposited. The attacker deposited 25,000 USDC through `prooflessDeposit`, registering one commitment, and then made 94 `transact` calls, 32 of which each withdrew 25,000 USDC. In total 797,000 USDC reached a single attacker address, roughly 31.9 times the deposit.

A notable feature of this incident is that the on-chain proof verifier was not bypassed. Tracing a withdrawal shows Hinkal ran a complete Groth16 verification (elliptic-curve linear combination over 14 public inputs followed by a pairing check) and accepted a distinct, well-formed proof for each of the 94 `transact` calls, every one carrying a unique nullifier. The failure is therefore not a disabled verifier and not a double-spend of one note; it is a break in value conservation inside the withdrawal circuit, reachable through the proofless deposit. The attacker was able to produce many independently valid withdrawal proofs against value that was never deposited.

The operation ran as a cluster of at least five coordinated EOAs, self-funded for gas and acting as their own relayers, with one address rehearsing the full exploit roughly five hours earlier. After the drain, the attacker swapped the 797,000 USDC to 454.24 WETH on Uniswap, unwrapped it to ETH, and laundered the proceeds within about 40 minutes: 410 ETH into Tornado Cash and 44.67 ETH bridged to native Bitcoin through THORChain.

Press figures of ~$800K to ~$820K correspond to the gross USDC that left the pool. The net loss to the protocol, after subtracting the attacker cluster's own 27,800 USDC of seed deposits, is approximately 772,400 USDC.

## 3. Attack vector and root cause

### 3.1 The two functions involved

Both functions are part of Hinkal's normal interface (verified proxy `0x25e5e82f5702A27C3466fE68f14abDbbAdFca826`, implementation `0x94c88fbf1a756012d405057ee6f3ea8578f28de1`):

- **`prooflessDeposit(address[] tokens, uint256[] amounts, uint256[] , (uint256,uint256,uint256,uint256)[] commitments)`** â€” selector `0x0ed4a94e`. A deposit path that pulls ERC-20 funds and inserts caller-supplied commitment notes into the shielded pool. As the name states, it does not carry a zero-knowledge proof.
- **`transact(uint256[2] a, uint256[2][2] b, uint256[2] c, â€¦)`** â€” selector `0x979a77a8`. The shielded spend/withdraw function. Its first arguments are a Groth16 proof (`a`, `b`, `c`), followed by the public inputs and routing parameters.

### 3.4 Root cause â€” a circuit soundness flaw (evidence-based)

The full `CircomData` struct carried by all 94 of the main attacker's `transact` calls settles what the defect is and is not. Across those 94 calls:

| Field | Observed | Meaning |
|---|---|---|
| `onChainCreation` | `false` in every slot of every call | The on-chain nullifier-skip path (see 3.4.1) was never used; the standard double-spend guard ran each time. |
| `inputNullifiers` | 94 distinct non-zero values | Each spend presented a fresh, never-seen nullifier, so the `"Nullifier cannot be reused"` check passed legitimately every time. Not replay. |
| `amountChanges` | `-25,000` USDC in 32 calls, `0` in 62 | 32 real withdrawals of 25,000 (=800,000 USDC); the rest carried no value. |
| `outCommitments` | `0` (no change note) on the withdrawals | Each 25,000 spend produced no offsetting output, so it was a pure exit. |
| `rootHashHinkal` | small set of known roots, all validated | Spends were proven against genuine Merkle roots. |

So the on-chain machinery all functioned correctly: the Groth16 verifier ran in full (3.3), nullifier uniqueness was enforced, roots were checked, and the deposit side conserved value (3.2). Yet 32 independently valid proofs each withdrew 25,000 USDC, with distinct nullifiers, against a single 25,000 USDC deposit. The flaw is in the zero-knowledge circuit itself.

**The commitment binds the amount, so the notes were not over-valued.** The leaf inserted into the tree is `createCommitment` = `hash4(amount, erc20Address, stealthAddress, timeStamp)` (Poseidon-4, or Poseidon-5 with a non-zero `tokenId`) â€” see `HinkalBase.sol`. The deposited amount is hashed into the leaf, so a note cannot claim more value than was deposited. Consistent with this, every withdrawal took exactly 25,000, the deposited figure.

**Event evidence: one note, spent many times.** The seed `prooflessDeposit` (tx `0xbf7252afâ€¦`) emitted exactly one `NewCommitment` and zero `Nullified`: one leaf, honestly worth 25,000. Each withdrawal (e.g. `0x93aeb876â€¦`) emitted one `Nullified` with a distinct nullifier and zero `NewCommitment`: it consumed a nullifier but created no new note. Across the 32 value-bearing withdrawals that is one deposited note converted into 32 distinct valid nullifications and 800,000 USDC out, with no new commitments and no change notes.

**Conclusion â€” an under-constrained nullifier.** The combination (amount bound in the leaf, sound standard verifier, distinct nullifier accepted each time, one note reused 32Ã—) points to the circuit failing to bind the nullifier uniquely to the specific note and its tree position. A correct design derives one deterministic nullifier per note, so on-chain uniqueness prevents a second spend. Here the circuit accepted many valid nullifiers for the same note, so the `"Nullifier cannot be reused"` guard was useless: every spend presented a fresh value it had never seen. The `prooflessDeposit` path is the enabler â€” it lets the attacker plant a note whose secrets they fully control, cheaply and without a formation proof, maximizing their ability to mint alternate valid nullifiers for it.

**Ruled out (on-chain evidence):** disabled or forged verifier (a full pairing check ran and passed, and the verifier is a standard snarkjs Groth16 contract â€” see 3.6); nullifier replay (all nullifiers distinct); the Solidity `onChainCreation` skip (flag was false throughout, 3.4.1); over-valued notes / deposit-side inflation (the leaf binds the amount and the deposit conserved value).

**What pinning the exact constraint would take (and why this report stops at the mechanism):** it does *not* require Hinkal's private Circom source. The constraint system is embedded in the proving key (`.zkey`), which Hinkal serves publicly (3.7), so the exact faulty constraint is recoverable by R1CS soundness analysis (for example Ecne or Picus) or by reconstructing the nullifier subcircuit from the artifacts. That is a heavier, specialized task than the on-chain forensics used here, which is why this report fixes the mechanism (an under-constrained nullifier) but not the single faulty line. The verifier bytecode alone cannot supply it: its verification key is a succinct trusted-setup commitment to the constraint system, not the constraints themselves (3.6).

#### 3.4.1 Why the `onChainCreation` skip did not apply

In Hinkal's core contract (`HinkalBase.sol`), `insertNullifiers` and `insertCommitments` both skip an input group when its `onChainCreation[i]` flag is set (`if (onChainCreation[i] == true) break;`), and that flag comes straight from attacker-supplied `circomData`. That looked like a candidate bug (a spender could skip nullification), but the calldata shows the attacker set it to `false` everywhere and instead relied on the circuit accepting fresh nullifiers. The double-spend guard was active and still did not stop the drain, which is what points the finger squarely at the circuit.

#### 3.4.2 How the keys bind a note, and where the binding broke

The following comes from Hinkal's public client SDK (`@hinkal/common` on npm): `keys.cjs`, `Utxo.cjs`, and the proof builder. It is fact except the final paragraph.

**Key hierarchy.** Everything derives from one wallet signature the user makes on first use:
```
nullifyingKey (nk) = keccak256(walletSignature)                  // long-lived shielded secret (scalar)
shieldedPublicKey  = poseidonHash(nk)
spendingKeyPair    = eddsa( poseidonHash(PREFIX, walletSignature) )   // BabyJubJub EdDSA keypair
randomization      = a fresh random scalar, one per note
```

**Stealth address (a per-note identity).** Built from two BabyJubJub curve points:
```
H0 = randomization Â· G          // per-note ephemeral point (G = base point)
H1 = nk Â· H0                    // the nullifying key applied to H0
stealthAddress = poseidonHash( signBits(H0,H1), H0.y, H1.y )                         // old style
               = poseidonHash( signBits, H0.y, H1.y, spendPub.x, spendPub.y, nk )    // new style
```
The load-bearing fact: `H1 = nk Â· H0` is the only thing tying `nk` to the note. Given `H0`, only the holder of `nk` can produce the matching `H1`, and `stealthAddress` commits to that pair.

**Commitment and nullifier.**
```
commitment = poseidonHash(amount, erc20Token, stealthAddress, timeStamp)   // the Merkle leaf (3.4)
nullifier  = poseidonHash(commitment, poseidonHash(nk, commitment))
```

**The binding a sound circuit must enforce.** When spending, the circuit takes `randomization`, `H0`, `amount`, and the Merkle path as private inputs and exposes `stealthAddress`, `H0`, the `nullifier`, and the root. Soundness needs the whole chain to hold at once:
```

```
If every link holds, a committed note pins its `nk` (the discrete log linking `H0` and `H1`, and in new style hashed into the leaf as well), so one note yields exactly one nullifier and the on-chain uniqueness check is effective.

**Where it broke.** `prooflessDeposit` lets the attacker supply `stealthAddress`, `H0`, and `H1` directly, with no proof that `H1 = nkÂ·H0` for any real `nk` (3.2); the contract simply hashes the amount into a leaf using the supplied `stealthAddress`. On-chain, one such note then produced 32 distinct valid nullifiers (3.4). Those two facts reconcile only if the withdrawal circuit does not force the `nk` inside the nullifier to be the key that satisfies `H1 = nkÂ·H0` for the committed note. With that link unconstrained, `nk` appears only in the nullifier and is otherwise free: the attacker holds the committed `(stealthAddress, H0, H1)` fixed so membership and the stealth-address check still pass, and supplies a different `nk` each time, so `nullifier_i = poseidon(commitment, poseidon(nk_i, commitment))` is a fresh valid value for every `nk_i`, all burning the same 25,000 note. The nullifying key that should be welded to the note came unglued from it.

**A deposit-time guarantee the spend side assumed.** This is why `prooflessDeposit` is the enabler rather than the bug itself. A proof-carrying deposit (`transact` with a positive amount change, 3.1) makes the circuit prove the new note is well-formed at mint, which forces `H1 = nkÂ·H0` and a properly derived `stealthAddress`. `prooflessDeposit` deliberately skips that mint-time proof, since its purpose is a cheap, no-proof deposit. The withdrawal circuit then appears to assume notes were already validated at mint and does not re-establish `H1 = nkÂ·H0` on spend. So the invariant held on the proof-carrying path and was silently absent for proofless notes: the classic gap where a cheaper entry point fails to re-check what the expensive one guaranteed.

**The binding was hardened once before.** The stealth address exists in two forms (above): the old style binds `nk` only through the curve relation `H1 = nkÂ·H0`, while the new style also hashes `nk` and the spending public key directly into `stealthAddress`. New style pins `nk` even against a lax circuit; old style does not. That Hinkal introduced new style at all shows the scheme was iterated and this binding tightened over time, consistent with (though not proof of) a proofless or old-style path never brought up to the same standard. Whether `prooflessDeposit` was in fact added after `transact`, and whether the gap was an oversight, is not verifiable from public artifacts: the repositories hold no readable history. This report asserts the mechanism, not the intent.

**Fact vs. hypothesis.** The key hierarchy, `H1 = nkÂ·H0`, the stealth-address hash, and the commitment and nullifier formulas are read directly from the public SDK; that the proofless note supplies these values directly, and that one note produced 32 distinct nullifiers, are on-chain facts. Open is *where* the missing check should have lived. Two candidates fit the evidence: (1) the withdrawal circuit does not re-derive `H1 = nkÂ·H0`, a pure circuit under-constraint; or (2) proofless notes were meant to be validated by the separate commitment-validation proof (`generateMainAndCommitmentZkProof`, or the `DepositOnChainUtxos` action) enforced off-chain by the relayer or enclave, and the attacker bypassed it by submitting its large withdrawals directly from its own EOA rather than through a relayer (4.2). Distinguishing these, and naming the exact faulty constraint (the `H1 = nkÂ·H0` weld, the EdDSA ownership check, or the stealth-address reconstruction), needs the labeled constraint system. The public `.zkey` (Groth16/BN254, 14 public inputs, ~74k constraints; 3.6) carries no signal names and the `.r1cs`/`.sym` are not served, so the exact constraint cannot be named without those files or a prover-level reproduction. The mechanism â€” an under-constrained nullifier reachable via `prooflessDeposit` â€” stands as the best-supported conclusion.

### 3.7 Did the attacker need the circuit source?

A natural inference is that exploiting a specific circuit under-constraint requires the private Circom source. It does not.

Producing a proof the verifier accepts requires the **proving key** and the **witness generator**, because the verifier is a standard sound Groth16 contract (3.6): proofs cannot be forged without them. So the attacker certainly had those proving artifacts. But having them does not imply source access, because Hinkal generates proofs **client-side** (the browser wallet builds each ZK proof), which means the proving key and witness generator are delivered to every user through the SDK. They are public by construction: the SDK (`@hinkal/common` on npm) fetches them from `https://wallet-prodv12.hinkal.io/verifiers-v2/` (Google Cloud Storage-backed, no authentication), one circuit per transaction shape â€” for example `mainEVMCircuit1x2x1-1.1.wasm` (5.1 MB) and `mainEVMCircuit1x2x1_final-1.1.zkey` (38.2 MB), whose `1x2x1` dimensions match the exploited withdrawals (3.3). The `.zkey` even carries the constraint coefficients themselves (its Coeffs section), so the constraint system is public in numeric form; only the signal-name map (`.sym`) and the human-readable `.circom` are withheld (3.6).

What exposes an under-constrained signal is the constraint system (R1CS), embedded in and derivable from those shipped artifacts; the under-constrained-signal tools in 8.5 analyze the compiled form, not the source.

Crucially, discovery and proof-crafting happen **off-chain and leave no trace**. An attacker assigns the nullifier an alternate value in the witness, generates the proof, and checks whether it verifies off-chain â€” against a local copy of the verifier or a forked chain state â€” all free, unlimited, and private, with no gas and no footprint. There is no reason to search for the bug on-chain, so the on-chain record does not (and would not) show that search. Consistent with this, the five-hour-early run on `0x105ad144â€¦` executed 190 `transact` calls with **zero reverts** (4.2): that reads as an end-to-end dry run of an exploit that already worked, not an on-chain search. The reverts that do appear elsewhere (17 on the sibling `0xc75d0b62â€¦`) are consistent with routine root/nullifier staleness and ordering failures during rapid scripted submission, not rejected proof guesses.

Takeaway: keeping the Circom repository private gave little protection. In any client-side-proving system the circuit is effectively public, so its soundness must be assured (8.5), not relied on to stay secret. This does not rule out an insider or a source leak, but nothing in the on-chain record requires one.

## 8. Remediation and recommendations

The confirmed defect is an under-constrained nullifier in the withdrawal circuit (Â§3.4), so the fixes below lead with that. The on-chain contracts, the verifier, and the deposit accounting were all sound, which means the on-chain measures are defense-in-depth, not the cure.

### 8.1 Immediate containment
- **Halt withdrawals.** `VerifierFacade.removeVerifier(verifierId)` and `registerVerifiers` are `onlyOwner`; removing or swapping the registered verifier stops `transact` at once. A pause guard on the entrypoint does the same. Until then the entire pool balance is exposed, not just one user's, because any depositor's backing can be withdrawn against a forged nullifier.
- **Quantify exposure.** Snapshot per-token pool balances versus outstanding legitimate deposits to bound the remaining loss, and notify depositors.

### 8.2 The core fix â€” constrain the nullifier in the circuit
This is the actual bug and the only change that closes it.
- Derive the nullifier as a deterministic function of the note and its unique tree position, and constrain it exactly: `nullifier === Poseidon(commitment, leafIndex, ownerSecret)` (or the scheme's equivalent). No free or under-determined witness signal may feed the nullifier; every input to it must be pinned by a constraint.
- Bind the **leaf index** (Merkle path) into the nullifier so a note at a fixed position yields exactly one nullifier. Reusing the note then collides on-chain and is rejected by the existing `"Nullifier cannot be reused"` check, which becomes effective again.
- Bind ownership: the secret that enters the nullifier must be the one that authorizes the note's `stealthAddress`, so a spender cannot compute a valid nullifier without actually owning the note.
- Add a circuit soundness test that asserts the property directly: for a fixed commitment and path, the constraint system must admit **exactly one** satisfying nullifier. Attempting to produce a second should be infeasible.
- After fixing the circuit, run a fresh trusted setup and redeploy the verifier, then point the registry at the new verifier id.

### 8.3 Harden the `prooflessDeposit` enabler
- The proofless path let the attacker insert a note whose secrets they fully control, with no proof of correct formation. Once the nullifier is properly constrained this is no longer exploitable, but as defense-in-depth require proofless-deposit commitments to carry a formation proof, or restrict them to a canonical construction the contract computes itself.
- Give commitments a dedicated high-entropy blinding factor. The current leaf `hash4(amount, erc20Address, stealthAddress, timeStamp)` uses `block.timestamp` as its only non-address entropy, which is predictable; a proper random salt reduces linkability and removes one more degree of attacker control over note structure.

### 8.4 What the contract layer can and cannot guard
A shielded pool deliberately moves the solvency-critical accounting into the circuit and hides it from the contract; that is what makes it private. The contract is blind to which note a nullifier spends or what it is worth, so it cannot re-derive per-user solvency without giving up unlinkability. That rules out a true on-chain cure for a circuit soundness bug, but several contract-layer measures bound the blast radius:

- **Weak floor â€” global solvency.** Tracking per-asset deposits minus withdrawals only stops the pool going negative, which the ERC-20 balance already enforces. It cannot tell a user withdrawing their own funds from an attacker draining everyone else's, and it trips only once the pool is nearly empty, so it caps loss at TVL and no lower. (This is why the exploit met no on-chain limit: it withdrew real backing that other depositors had put in.)
- **Effective â€” withdrawal velocity limits and timelocks.** Cap withdrawals per epoch (a percentage of pool balance) and delay large or rapid exits. The entire 800,000 USDC left in about 16 minutes; a per-hour cap or a timelock on outsized withdrawals turns a total drain into a partial loss and leaves time to detect the anomaly and remove the verifier (8.1). This is the strongest practical on-chain mitigation.
- **Surface reduction â€” constrain proofless deposits.** Since the proofless path is what let the attacker cheaply plant a fully-controlled note, cap value spendable against proofless notes, require them to mature before being spendable, or require a formation proof.
- **Soundness-targeting â€” redundant verification.** The one contract-layer defense that addresses an unknown circuit bug directly: require each withdrawal to satisfy two independently authored circuits (or two proving systems) for the same statement, and verify both on-chain. An under-constraint in one is unlikely to exist in the other. It roughly doubles proving and verification cost, which is why it is reserved for high-value pools.

The tradeoff is fundamental: full privacy and full contract-layer guardability are in tension. A contract that could verify per-note solvency could also link spends to deposits. The realistic posture is a sound circuit (8.2) plus coarse aggregate guards (velocity, timelocks) that limit how much a future soundness failure can take.

### 8.5 Assurance and process
- Commission a dedicated **zero-knowledge circuit audit**. The Solidity was audited (Quantstamp), but the flaw is in the circuit, which is a different discipline and specialist scope.
- Run under-constrained-signal analysis in CI. Tools such as circomspect, Ecne, and Picus are built to detect exactly this class of bug (an output signal not uniquely determined by the inputs).
- Publish a transparent post-mortem and the corrected circuit, and size the bug bounty to the pool's TVL so this class of finding is reported rather than exploited.

### 8.6 Monitoring (applies to any shielded pool)
- A single account turning a small deposit into a large, repeated, same-size withdrawal within minutes is a strong exploit signature. Per-account withdraw-to-deposit ratio and burst withdrawal counts are cheap alerts.
- A rehearsal run against the live contract hours ahead (here, about five hours on `0x105ad144â€¦`) is itself a detection opportunity.
- The immediate hop to Tornado Cash and a THORChain to BTC swap is now a standard cash-out for mid-size DeFi thefts; fast mixer and cross-chain monitoring shortens the window in which funds are freezable, which here was effectively zero.

### 8.7 Recovery
- After pausing and shipping the circuit fix, quantify affected depositors and publish a reimbursement plan. Redeploying without a corrected circuit and a new trusted setup would reopen the same hole.

## 9. Broader context

The exploited primitive here is a smart-contract soundness flaw, distinct from the account-compromise and front-end supply-chain incidents that dominated much of 2026. It is a reminder that privacy protocols concentrate risk: a single accounting gap in a shielded pool converts directly into a drain of the whole pool's backing, and the same privacy design that protects users also gives the attacker a ready-made laundering exit.
